
## Journal of Ilia Valev, House IV

_Ore Processing and Records_
### **Entry I — The Stranger in Green**

I noticed him first because the lamps adjusted.

That sounds foolish written down, but it is true. When he passed beneath the lights near the chapel, the lumen strips dipped, then steadied again, as if compensating for something heavier than expected.

He did not arrive with a transport. At least we heard no engine noise, or dust plume. He was simply there one evening, standing beside the chapel doors while the bells were being shut down for the night. He waited patiently, like someone who understood shifts and schedules.

He asked for work.
Not shelter or food. Work.

We gave him the worst of it. That is how these things go. Deep shafts, old cuts, places where the maps contradict themselves and the stone remembers older pressures. He accepted without hesitation.

It didnt take long for people to start talking.

Tunnels held where they usually sloughed. Machinery that should have seized ran another shift. He carried loads that should have required two men and did so without comment or strain. He worked longer hours than anyone and never seemed exhausted.

He wore green, but not any uniform I recognize. The fabric was heavy, layered, scorched in places. Beneath it was armor. I saw it once when his sleeve tore—dark, scarred, deliberately stripped of all markings. Whoever he was, he did not want to be recognized.

A child asked him if he was an angel.

He paused longer than necessary, then answered:

> “An angel of death.”

The child laughed, thinking it a joke.

I did not.

### **Entry II — The Night Below**

The disappearances were not sudden. That is what frightened me most.

One miner late for a shift. Another found dead where the rockfall should not have killed him. Scratches in places no one could reach. Sounds in the deep that did not echo correctly.

We blamed fatigue. Light deprivation. Stress. As we always do.

He stopped taking regular shifts and began asking questions instead. About old tunnels. About sections sealed before the settlement was complete. About the caves that predated us entirely.

No one told him to stop.

The night it happened, the ground shook—but not like a collapse. It felt restrained. Controlled. As if something enormous had been forced back into stillness.

The vox died. The lamps flickered. The chapel bells rang once, without being touched.

He took one of the votive lanterns from the chapel—not Saint Dravik’s relic, but one kept burning in his honor—and went below. A few miners followed him at first. He turned them back with a single look.

They listened.

Hours passed. No sound. No tremor. No screaming. That was worse.

When he returned, his robes were torn and blackened. Beneath them I saw more clearly than before: armor fused to flesh, scars layered upon scars, a body built not for labor but for annihilation.

His sword was no longer wrapped.

He did not speak of what he fought.

The disappearances ended that night.

### **Entry III — The Departure and the Mark**

He left without announcement.

No farewell. No demand for thanks. He rose before shift change and walked toward the forests where the twilight never lifts properly. No one followed him. I think we all understood that following would be a mistake.

By midday, someone noticed the chapel wall.

The carving had not been there before. I am certain of it—I have catalogued every crack and stain in that building.

A hooded figure. Wings folded, not spread. A sword held point-down, resting. Not victorious. Not defeated. Watchful.

It was not placed at the altar. It did not challenge Saint Dravik. It stood beside the door, as if acknowledging a different kind of duty.

When asked why he carved it, the chapel keeper told me the words he left behind:

> “Dravik carried the light into the dark.  
> He stood where the dark tried to come through.”

They did not pray to the carving.
They did not name him.
They hung it anyway.

Not as a saint. Not as an angel but as a reminder.

That sometimes salvation does not come gently.  
That sometimes it wears green, carries a sword, and leaves before anyone asks too much.

If the Imperium ever asks, this never happened.

But the tunnels remember.  
And so do we.

---

### **Entry IV — Before We Leave**

I am writing this by lantern, not because the power is gone, but because the lantern light still feels honest.

We are packing what little makes sense to take. Records first. Tools second. Everything else is being argued over, then abandoned. That is how endings happen here—slowly, then all at once.

The chapel is still standing.

It should not be. Half the settlement has failed inspection. Roofs sag. Walls sweat mineral salts. But the chapel’s stones have held better than anything else we built. The electrocandles burn steadily, even when the load fluctuates.

Saint Dravik stands there unchanged. His lantern still glows a little warmer than it should. I no longer question that.

The other mark is still there too.

The Angel’s insignia has not cracked or weathered. Dust gathers around it, but never on it. When the light is wrong—which is often now—it almost seems to draw the shadow toward itself, as if insisting on where it belongs.

People stopped talking about him years ago.

Not because they forgot, but because remembering became uncomfortable. The light here does things to memory. It softens edges. Makes stories feel optional. I catch myself wondering if I imagined parts of it.

Then I stand in front of that carving and my doubt settles.

We never named him. That was deliberate. Names invite questions, and questions invite attention. I taught my children only this:

> He came when we needed him and he left before it became dangerous to stay.

I sometimes wonder if he is still alive.
If he remembers us at all.

We are leaving because the light has worn us thin. Because the settlement feels hollow in ways no reinforcement can fix. Because some places are not meant to be endured forever.

Before we go, I will extinguish the chapel lamps myself, one by one, except for the votive by the carving. That one I will leave burning. Not as a prayer.

As a courtesy.

If the Angel ever returns—and I do not know why he would—I want him to know that we remembered the difference.

Between a saint who carried the light.
And an Angel of Death who stood in the dark so we could walk away.
