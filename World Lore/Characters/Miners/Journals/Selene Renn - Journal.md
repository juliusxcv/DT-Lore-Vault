### Rations Adjustements
Rations balanced for another cycle. We are doing better than the projections suggested, mostly because people have stopped wasting food. Hard to say if that’s discipline or appetite.

There was an argument at breakfast about shift swaps. Nothing serious. Voices raised, then laughter. It’s good to hear laughter again, even when it comes from irritation.

I’ve started writing names on supply crates instead of just counts. It slows things down, but it helps when something goes missing. Accountability makes people calmer.

### There’s a feeling I don’t like.
That sounds childish, but I don’t know how else to phrase it. The schedules still balance. Supplies still arrive. People still laugh at meals.

And yet decisions feel heavier. Like each one is being weighed against something I can’t see.

We had a discussion today about whether we should slow expansion. It ended without resolution. No one wanted to be the first to say it aloud, but everyone was thinking the same thing:

What if we were wrong to come here?

We have never asked that question before.

---
### We held a meeting today.
It lasted eleven minutes. That might be a record. Everyone said the same things they said last week, just more quietly.

No one wants to say “leave.” No one wants to say “stay.”

Supplies are being packed more carefully now. Not to conserve—just in case. That frightens me more than panic would.

People are starting to sit with their backs to walls at meals.

I’ve stopped asking why.

---
### People are speaking again.
Not loudly. Not easily. But the silence has changed. It’s no longer the kind that presses on your ears. It feels like a pause instead of a warning.

No one has asked the stranger where he came from. That feels deliberate. As if asking might break whatever fragile balance has returned.

He helped move crates this morning. Heavy ones. He moved slowly, carefully, like he was afraid of startling someone.

That sounds ridiculous.
And yet.

---
### Crates, Crates, Crates
The quiet one volunteered for perimeter crate transport today.

I reassigned him twice because I assumed he’d get tired. He didn’t. He moved slower than the others, but never stopped.

He doesn’t talk much, but when he does, people listen. Not because he’s loud—he isn’t—but because he doesn’t waste words.

When two workers argued over crate counts, he stepped between them without raising his voice and said, “You’re both correct. The error is earlier.”

He was right.
That ended the argument.

Addendum: Corvin drives me insane sometimes. He just confessed to me that he apparently misplaced the Vault Key in one of the crates. Good Luck retrieving them after the Big Guy moved all of them. He supposedly marked it with our Family Crest.

### A good Day, i guess
Everyone worked faster today.

No arguments. No hesitation. Fewer mistakes. It felt like the end of a long illness—you don’t remember when the fever broke, only that suddenly you can breathe again.

I reorganized the transport schedule without resistance. People agreed more easily than usual. I don’t think it was discipline. I think they were tired of tension and grateful for its absence.

At midday I realized no one had volunteered for the outer crate run.

He always did.

I reassigned it and felt strangely apologetic, like I was asking someone to fill a space that wasn’t meant to be filled.
