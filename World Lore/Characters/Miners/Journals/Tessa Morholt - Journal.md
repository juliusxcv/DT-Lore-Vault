### Medicae Report
Minor injuries today: crushed finger, two lacerations, one stress migraine. All treated. No complications.

Sleep complaints remain common. I’ve advised staggered rest periods, but people resist changing routines once they’ve set in. Familiar discomfort feels safer than new solutions.

Someone left a bundle of glow-sticks outside the medicae room as thanks. I don’t know who. I’ve redistributed them to the night crews.

---
### Stress symptoms increasing.

More headaches. More shallow sleep. More small injuries that come from inattention rather than hazard.

People don’t talk about fear directly. They talk about being tired. I let them. Sometimes naming the wrong thing is better than naming the right one too early.

One patient asked me today if the disappearances were “done.”

I told him I hoped so.

I shouldn’t have said that. Hope isn’t treatment.

---
### I am seeing the same symptoms repeatedly.
Insomnia. Tremors. Heightened startle response. People flinching at nothing and apologizing for it.

We are running out of sedatives faster than planned. I have started reducing dosages to stretch supply. That will make things worse. I know that.

One patient died today.

Officially, heart failure.

I held his hand while the light from the corridor spilled into the room at the wrong angle and made his shadow stretch across the wall in a way that felt… deliberate.

I closed the door.

---
### No deaths today.
That sentence looks strange on the page.

Fewer tremors. Fewer panic responses. People still look tired, but the exhaustion feels earned now, not inflicted.

The stranger brought in a worker with a crushed foot this afternoon. He stayed while I treated it, completely still, watching my work as if memorizing it.

Before leaving, he said, “He’ll keep it.”

He was right.

### Medicae Report
He brought in a worker with a crushed hand today. From what he told me that happened, the injury should have been worse. The bones were misaligned, but not shattered.

I asked how it happened. He said, “I was there first.”

That was all.

He stood perfectly still while I worked, watching my hands instead of the wound. When I finished, he nodded once, like a patient might, and left with the injured worker.

I realized afterward that he never asked if the worker would recover.

Perhaps he already knew.

---
### All is well
No emergencies today.

I am writing that carefully, as if the words might overhear me.

The injured worker from yesterday returned for a checkup. Healing well. Better than expected. I didn’t say that aloud.

When I finished, he asked if the tall one would be back.

I told him I didn’t know.

That was the truth.

I cleaned the medicae room longer than necessary afterward. Put things back where they already were. It helped. I also hid emergency stims behind the panel (2x MedStims)
