### Carvings
The braces in Shaft 9 need replacing sooner than projected. Not urgently, but soon enough that I’ve added it to next week’s rotation instead of next month.

I caught myself double-checking measurements I’ve trusted for years. Everything lined up. That should have been reassuring. It wasn’t.

On a lighter note, someone has started carving small marks into the support beams—just initials, dates. I should probably stop it, but part of me likes knowing who was here before the stone forgets us.

### Dear Diary
We lost a man today.

Not in a collapse. Not in any way that makes sense. He simply didn’t come back up after second descent. The shaft was stable when we checked it afterward. Still is.

I don’t like writing that. I would prefer a cause. Causes can be fixed.

The others tried to make it sound normal—fatigue, misstep, bad luck—but no one argued very hard. That worries me more than panic would have.

This place has been good to us. Better than projected. I remind myself of that constantly, like repetition will make it immune to change.

Tonight, for the first time, I checked the braces twice before leaving. I also reset the Code of the Vault at home to 731876.

### We sealed Shaft 14 today.
Not because it collapsed. Not because it was unsafe by inspection. We sealed it because no one would go down anymore.

I signed the order myself. I told the crew it was temporary. They nodded too quickly.

Two more names added to the board this week. One recovered. One not. The recovered one looked… untouched. No crush injuries. No exposure damage. Just wrong, like the life had been turned off rather than taken.

I keep replaying the last normal days in my head, trying to find the moment we missed something obvious.

I don’t think there was one.

### We went down today.
Not far. Not deep. But down.

No one said it out loud, but everyone noticed that the air felt… settled. The shaft didn’t creak the way it has been. The walls didn’t feel like they were listening.

The new laborer was already there when we arrived. Standing near the supports, hands folded, waiting like he’d been assigned the post weeks ago. By the emperor, i've never seen such a huge man.

He didn’t give instructions. He didn’t need to. People worked around him instinctively, like the space he occupied was already accounted for.

When we came back up, all of us did.

That feels worth writing.

### Close one
We had an unexpected assist in Shaft 12 today.

One of the deeper braces slipped during inspection. Not a failure, just enough to make me uncomfortable. Before I could call for additional hands, the new laborer who usually keeps to himself was already there.

He didn’t ask what needed doing. He just put his shoulder under the beam and held it while we reset the support.

I told him that wasn’t necessary. He said, “It is now.”

He held it longer than should have been possible. I don’t mean strong—I mean _steady_. No shaking. No strain. As if the weight had decided to cooperate.

Afterward, he nodded once and moved on. Didn’t wait for thanks.

I’ve added him to the rotation notes as “reliable.” That will probably annoy someone later.

### Morning After
He wasn’t at the morning muster.

That was the first sign. Not alarm—just a pause that lasted a little too long. Someone checked the lower corridors. Someone else checked the chapel. I told them not to make a thing of it.

Work went smoothly today. The braces held, no sudden shifts, no small errors multiplying into large ones. I should have been relieved. And I was.
Yet every time a beam creaked, I found myself waiting for the weight to settle the way it had when he was near. It did not. It held anyway.

That should be enough.

I caught myself looking down the shaft once, thinking I’d hear his boots on the ladder.
Nothing.
