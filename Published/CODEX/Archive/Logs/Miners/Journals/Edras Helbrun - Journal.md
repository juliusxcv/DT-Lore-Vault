### Field Report / Personal Log
I am combining this report with my personal log because the distinction is becoming artificial.

The ore is still there.

That is the problem.

Initial surveys promised a steady return curve once the primary veins were opened. Instead, we are seeing fragmentation—thin, discontinuous seams that do not behave like deposits formed under stable pressure. The scanners still light up. The drills still bite. But the yield per meter falls every quarter, sometimes every week.

We are extracting _stone_, not value.

I have recalibrated the atmospheric and geological sensors twice. The readings are consistent, which would be reassuring if they made sense. Subsurface pressure is not dropping in response to extraction. In some zones, it appears to be redistributing laterally, as if the moon were compensating for us.

That should not happen.

We, the Helbruns, spend more time outside than most, and I am beginning to understand why others avoid it. The light here does not change enough to mark time. Shadows stretch too far and then pull back. Distance lies.

On my last survey run, I walked a ridgeline I have walked a dozen times before and misjudged its end by nearly three meters. That error should not be possible. I checked my markers. They had not moved.

The company projections assume that diminishing returns are temporary. That once we breach deeper, the yields will normalize.

I am no longer confident.

The deeper we go, the less the moon behaves like an object and the more it behaves like a system reacting to intrusion. This is not superstition. This is pattern recognition.

I have advised reducing expansion and consolidating existing shafts. The response was polite and noncommittal. Everyone here wants this to work. I understand that. I want it too.

But feasibility is not a feeling.

It is math.

And the math is turning against us.

One more thing, which I hesitate to include:

The ore itself has changed. Not compositionally—spectral analysis is within expected variance—but _structurally_. It fractures along planes that do not align with known stress vectors. When struck, some samples produce vibrations that persist longer than they should, like a bell rung too softly.

I have caught myself standing still, listening to stone after the drills stop.

That is not professional.

I will file this formally tomorrow. For now, this entry remains personal.

If we cannot justify the operation economically within the next cycle, someone will make the decision for us. It will not be gentle.

I find myself hoping we leave before the moon decides to tell us why it does not want to be opened further.
