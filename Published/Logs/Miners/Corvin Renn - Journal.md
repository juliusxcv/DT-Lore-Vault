### Walking Home
I am recording this because sleep will not come, and because if I do not put it into words it will grow larger than it deserves.

This is not a sighting.  
This is not folklore.  
This is fatigue.

We have been running double shifts since the last ore lift fell behind schedule, and the light here—if you can call it that—does no one any favors. There is no proper dark, no proper day. Just that endless wash of color that makes shadows behave like liquid.

I took the perimeter route this evening instead of cutting through the lower service corridor. Fresh air, such as it is. I should not have.

The forest was quiet in the way it sometimes is, when even the insects seem to pause. I remember thinking that the silence felt _organized_, which is ridiculous and only proves my point about exhaustion.

I saw movement between the trees.

Not directly. That is important. When I turned my head, there was nothing there. When I looked away, something shifted. A distortion, like heat haze, but wrong for the temperature. Taller than a man. Thinner. Too vertical to be brush or shadow.

I stopped walking. That was my first mistake.

There was no fear at first. Just curiosity. Then a pressure in my chest, like standing too close to heavy machinery. I could feel a vibration through the soles of my boots, faint but steady, as if the ground itself were humming.

I told myself it was the generators.

Then my bones started to ache.

Not pain—recognition. As if something were resonating through me rather than at me. That was when I laughed, out loud, because that is exactly the sort of nonsense people start believing right before they embarrass themselves.

When I blinked, it was gone.

Of course it was.

I stood there for a long time afterward, angry at myself. Angry enough to feel steadier. The forest returned to normal sounds. The light stopped behaving strangely. My heartbeat slowed.

There are stories the older workers tell about things in the woods. I have never indulged that talk. Isolation and bad light explain everything those stories describe.

I will submit a request to rotate perimeter duty. Not because of fear—because of efficiency. No sense risking accidents due to fatigue-induced hallucinations.

Still.

I do not like how quiet the trees were before I saw it.
I do not like that I felt watched without feeling threatened.
And I do not like how relieved I was when it chose to leave.

This entry will not be shared.

Tomorrow I will sleep properly and this will feel foolish.

It must.
